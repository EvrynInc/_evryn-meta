## Critical Issue

**Privacy Notice, Profiling section:**

The language reads:

> "We do not make any decisions about individuals, and we do not tell Initiating User whether or not to reach out to a particular Non-initiating User."

**This is factually wrong.** Evryn's entire Phase 1 service is classifying contacts as Match / Edge Case / Pass and telling Mark which ones are worth his time. That is *literally* making decisions about individuals and telling the Initiating User who to reach out to. The Redirect Decisions defined in the ToS (Section 1) are exactly this.

The intent is understandable - creating distance from GDPR Article 22 "significant effects" triggers - but they overcorrected into a statement that contradicts the service description in their own ToS. If a Non-initiating User ever challenges Evryn ("you classified me as a Pass and I never got surfaced"), this sentence would be used against them: "You said you don't make decisions about individuals, but you classified me and that classification determined whether I got surfaced."

**Recommendation:** Revise to something that's both accurate and GDPR-safe. Fenwick already agreed to this framing in their March 24 response (item 13): acknowledge that automated assessments meaningfully influence service delivery, note that all classifications are subject to human review before action is taken, and retain "they do not produce legal or similarly significant effects on individuals." That framing was correct; the new Profiling language overshoots it.

## Important Issues

### 1. ALL CAPS on new sections (13c, 13d, 13e)

Our March 22 addendum specifically asked whether bold could replace ALL CAPS for conspicuous language. Sections 13(c) "No Guarantee of Matching," 13(d) "No Professional Advice," and 13(e) "No Referral Relationship" are all in ALL CAPS. The warranty disclaimers and arbitration in ALL CAPS are standard/expected, but these new sections are substantive business terms, not warranty disclaimers - they don't have the same legal requirement for conspicuous formatting. Worth raising on the call.

### 2. Data license - no anonymization language (item 3)

We specifically asked for language like "commercially reasonable steps to anonymize and de-identify data used for model improvement." Section 9(b)(iii) still just says "to fine-tune Evryn's machine learning models" with no data protection language. The "publicly display/perform" removal is good, but the model training commitment we wanted is missing.

### 3. Data deletion email address inconsistency

ToS Section 9(c) says: "You can remove your Customer Email Data by emailing support@evryn.ai." Our March 22 addendum specified **privacy@evryn.ai** for data deletion. The Privacy Notice correctly uses privacy@evryn.ai in the Contact Us section, so there's an inconsistency between the two documents.

### 4. Typos

- ToS crisis language: "to in the event Evryn learns" - should be "in the event"
- Privacy Notice: "non-reversible salted hashed" - should be "salted hash"

## Items from March 27 Email to Address on the Call

### 1. Phase/version terminology

They asked for clarification. Easy: Phase 1 = v0.2, Phase 2 = v0.3.

### 2. Wireframes/mockups for Phase 2

They said "which we imagine you will have in place if launch is as imminent as described." We don't have wireframes - the interface is primarily conversational (chat). Offer to demo the current email-based flow and describe the web app architecture verbally. The "product team" they want to involve is Justin.

### 3. Outreach consent requirement

This is a real and important legal point. Non-initiating Users need to assent to Terms before Evryn proceeds with them as users. This has product implications for the v0.3 build - the outreach flow needs a consent step.

Their suggested auto-reply language ("Mark is not interested in meeting at this time") is **completely wrong** for Evryn's model - that's not what's happening, and it would be terrible UX. But the underlying requirement (consent before proceeding) is valid and needs to be designed into the outreach flow. Discuss on the call, but push back hard on the suggested language.

### 4. Over-disclosure principle

Their explanation is sound and well-articulated. The key sentence: "what you say you will do, you must do." This is good advice. Accept the principle; push on specific applications where disclosure is warranted (which they've already agreed to on the two items we flagged).
