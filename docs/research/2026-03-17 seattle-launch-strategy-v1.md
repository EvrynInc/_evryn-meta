## The Headline

Evryn doesn't need to conquer Seattle. Seattle is already desperate for exactly what Evryn is.

The city is the loneliest, most depressed major metro in the country — full of transplants who can't make friends, tech workers who just got laid off, and communities that are rich internally but completely blind to each other. Everyone is trying to solve this with events and apps and meetups, and none of it is working because the tools are all wrong. Evryn is the right tool. And because Seattle is a throughput city — people constantly arriving and leaving — every user Evryn wins here becomes a seed somewhere else.

This report maps where the water is, how to drink it, and why Seattle isn't just a launch city — it's a launchpad.

## Part 1: The Landscape

### Seattle in 2026: A City of Contradictions

Seattle is simultaneously one of the wealthiest and loneliest cities in America. Understanding this contradiction is essential to understanding why Evryn fits here.

**The economy is bifurcating.** The startup ecosystem is healthy — 5th strongest in the US, $2.52B in total funding, 11% annual growth, 75+ active VC firms with $8B+ in assets. AI/ML captured 65%+ of all VC deal value in 2025. But big tech is contracting hard: 30,000+ tech job cuts announced in the Seattle area in 2025. Amazon alone cut 14,000 corporate roles (2,303 hit Washington state). Microsoft shed 3,200+ WA jobs since May 2025. Meta cut ~330 Seattle roles in January 2026. Seattle leads the world in AI-driven layoffs — 1 in 5 of 45,000+ global AI-related cuts happened here. Unemployment hit 5.0-5.1%, above the national 4.5%.

**The city is expensive and getting more unequal.** Median household income: ~$121,984. Median home price: ~$785,000-$850,000. Cost of living 45-50% above the national average. Housing specifically is 111% above. The wealthiest fifth of households earn 21x the poorest fifth — up from 19x in 2019, and BCG predicts Seattle will pass New York in inequality within 20 years. A single person needs ~$135K/year to "live comfortably" (6th highest in the US).

**The population is growing, but only from immigration.** City population: 816,600 (crossed 800K in 2025). Metro: 3.58M. But domestic migration is net negative — more Americans are leaving Seattle than arriving. Growth is entirely driven by international immigration: +64,000 to the metro area annually, offset by ~11,000 domestic out-migration. Only 35% of residents were born in Washington — the lowest in nearly 100 years. Median age: 35.6. Millennials: 42% of the population.

**The downtown is hollowing.** Office vacancy hit a record 34.7-35.6% in Q4 2025, with 2.3 million square feet vacated during the year. Some signs of bottoming out, but the recovery will be slow. Meanwhile, restaurants are churning: 45 new openings from November 2025 to January 2026, but closures continue. Business owners cite rising minimum wage ($20.76/hr), food costs, rent, and crime.

**People are arriving, and people are leaving.** This will matter enormously for Evryn's growth model (see Part 3: The Spore Dynamic).

## Part 2: Seven Waters

The demand for trusted connection in Seattle runs through seven distinct but interconnected channels. They're listed in order of how acutely they align with what Evryn does.

### Water 1: The Freeze Itself

Seattle's core social dysfunction — not a bug, but a feature of the city's culture that creates structural demand for exactly what Evryn offers.

**Why it's water for Evryn:** The Freeze is a collective action problem. Everyone wants connection; nobody initiates. Traditional solutions (events, apps, meetups) require the user to overcome the Freeze first — to show up, to swipe, to introduce themselves. Evryn doesn't require any of that. She comes to you. She initiates. She remembers. She follows up. In a city where 40% of people say making new friends "isn't important" (a defense mechanism, not a genuine preference — per the 2026 study), Evryn bypasses the defense by not asking for anything the city can't give. She just starts being your friend.

**Who this serves:** Everyone, but especially transplants (see Water 2), single adults (49% of residents 20+), remote workers (24% of the workforce — people who've lost the primary social infrastructure of the office), and young adults (50% reporting loneliness).

### Water 2: The Transplant Flood

78% of Seattle's population growth is net migration. 64,000 international migrants arrive annually. Only 35% of residents were born in Washington. These people arrive with zero local networks into what the data says is one of the hardest cities in America to make friends.

**Why it's water for Evryn:** Transplants need *everything* — a doctor, a plumber, a friend, a gym, a climbing partner, a date, a cofounder, a babysitter. They need it across multiple domains simultaneously. And they're arriving into a city that won't help them find any of it organically. This is Evryn's entire value proposition in one demographic.

**The immigrant layer:** King County has 585,000 foreign-born residents (25.6% of the population). The top communities — Indian (~83K), Chinese (~80K), Mexican (~55K), plus Vietnamese, Filipino, Ethiopian, Ukrainian, Brazilian, Somali, and others — are served by heroic organizations (IRC, ReWA, ACRS, Casa Latina, El Centro de la Raza, Ethiopian Community in Seattle, and many more). But these organizations focus on survival needs: housing, legal, language. *Connection* — finding your people, building a professional network, finding work above the entry level, finding a community that feels like home — is a massive unmet need above the survival layer. Evryn isn't competing with these organizations. She's serving the need they don't have infrastructure for.

**Scale:** At 64,000 international arrivals per year plus domestic inbound migration, the transplant population replenishes constantly. This is a market that never dries up.

### Water 3: Tech Displacement

30,000+ tech job cuts in Seattle in 2025. Seattle leads the world in AI-driven layoffs. These are highly skilled, well-connected, well-compensated people who suddenly need to find their next thing.

**Why it's water for Evryn:** Career transition is one of the most acute connection needs in any city. Displaced tech workers need introductions to startups (the ecosystem is healthy — $2.52B in funding), to other industries, to cofounders, to mentors. They need to *reimagine* what they do and who they know. And they have money — median household income is $121,984; the tech cohort skews much higher. These are people who will pay real money for connections that actually work.

**The startup matching gap:** Seattle's startup ecosystem is the 5th strongest in the US, but consumer startups are only 15% of seed rounds (down from 35% in 2020). The ecosystem skews heavily B2B SaaS and enterprise. A displaced tech worker who wants to join or start a consumer company needs to find the small community of people doing that — and current networking events are described as "schmoozy and disingenuous" by multiple sources. The invite-only community Foundations exists precisely because open networking doesn't work well. Evryn does what Foundations does, but for everyone.

**The AI transition specifically:** Seattle is ground zero for AI-driven job displacement. The workers being replaced understand AI — they're not scared of it, they're living it. An AI broker that helps them find their next chapter is poetically fitting and practically essential.

### Water 4: The Cross-Ecosystem Void

Every community ecosystem in Seattle — film, tech, food, music, outdoor, fitness, nonprofit, art, literary, gaming — is dense internally but almost completely siloed from the others. The chef who climbs, the filmmaker who codes, the nonprofit leader who brews beer — these people exist, but there is no infrastructure for the intersections.

**Why it's water for Evryn:** This is where Evryn's cross-domain model isn't just an advantage — it's the thing nobody else is even attempting. Every other tool serves one community: Meetup.com for events, LinkedIn for professional, dating apps for romance, Yelp for services. Nobody connects the filmmaker who needs a plumber with the plumber who wants to be in a film. Nobody connects the displaced tech worker who loves climbing with the startup founder they'd meet at the climbing gym if they only knew to look.

**Seattle's unique cross-domain density:** The city is small enough that people's *lives* are cross-domain in ways that LA's aren't. The same person is at the climbing gym Monday, a tech meetup Tuesday, volunteering Wednesday, an indie show Thursday. In a large, sprawling city, these identities don't overlap. In Seattle, they do — but the infrastructure doesn't see it. Evryn can.

**The data from every ecosystem confirms it:**
- Film: "There's no central hub where a filmmaker looking for a DP, an actor looking for indie projects, or a producer looking for a screenwriter can efficiently find each other."
- Tech: "A first-time founder without big-tech alumni connections can struggle to find the right mentor or cofounder."
- Food: "The community is warm but closed — you get in by working in it, not by seeking it out."
- Music: "There's no efficient way for a drummer looking for a band, or a producer looking for vocalists, to find each other beyond Craigslist and word-of-mouth."
- Fitness: "The fitness community is highly siloed — your CrossFit gym doesn't talk to the yoga studio next door."
- Outdoor: "Finding partners for specific activities at specific skill levels is still hard."
- Nonprofit: "Small nonprofits are isolated — they compete for the same grants and don't share infrastructure."
- Art: "There's no efficient way for artists to find collaborators across disciplines."

The same structural problem, repeated across every ecosystem. One tool that spans all of them changes everything.

### Water 5: Dating Desperation

Seattle has the 2nd highest online dating usage among large metros, in-person dating events more than doubled from 2022-2024, and app fatigue is widespread. 120.5 unmarried men per 100 unmarried women under 45. 49% of adults 20+ are unmarried. The dating experience is defined by "dating app fatigue, ghosting, and the Seattle Freeze" (Axios, January 2026).

**Why it's water for Evryn:** Evryn isn't a dating app — but romantic connection is absolutely one of her domains. And the competitive landscape is wide open: the incumbents (Bumble #1 in Seattle, Hinge, Tinder) are generating active fatigue. People are *spending money* on in-person dating events trying to escape the apps. Evryn offers something none of them do: a trusted friend who knows you across your whole life, not just your dating profile. "I know you're looking for a partner, but I also know you just started climbing and you're looking for a job change — and I met someone last week who checks all three boxes." No dating app can do that.

**The gender imbalance creates asymmetric demand.** 120 men per 100 women means men have to work harder and women have more options but worse signal-to-noise ratio. Evryn serves both sides: she filters for men (your quality stands out when it's not buried in a swipe stack) and she curates for women (only people Evryn trusts enough to connect you to).

### Water 6: The Practical Needs Layer

Everyone in Seattle needs specific kinds of people. Contractors (old housing stock, everyone renovating), specialists (tech wealth + specific tastes), childcare providers, healthcare practitioners, creative collaborators. Word-of-mouth is how people find service providers — but the Freeze makes word-of-mouth hard. People don't trust Yelp or Angi. They want a recommendation from someone they trust, but they don't know enough people to ask.

**Why it's water for Evryn:** The supply-side/batching economics (detailed in the business-model spoke) map perfectly to Seattle. When 50 people need a plumber, Evryn finds the 5 best and connects them to all 50. One search, many connections. API cost stays flat while revenue scales linearly. At ~$3,250/mo steady-state operating cost, 200 plumber connections at $20 each = $4,000. One category of connection covers the entire monthly operating cost.

**This also feeds the cross-ecosystem insight:** The plumber Evryn recruits doesn't just serve 50 plumbing needs — they become a full Evryn user with their own needs (a date, a climbing partner, a financial advisor). The practical needs layer is an acquisition engine for every other layer.

### Water 7: Anti-Corporate Trust-First Resonance

Seattle houses Amazon and Microsoft, but the broader culture is deeply skeptical of what those companies represent. People here understand surveillance capitalism viscerally — many of them helped build it. They're allergic to being monetized and responsive to being trusted.

**Why it's water for Evryn:** Three specific resonance points:

**Trust-based pricing speaks the local language.** Seattle birthed REI (a co-op). Strong farmers market culture. Strong "support local" identity. Progressive economic experiments ($15 minimum wage pioneer). "Pay what you feel is right" maps to a culture that understands paying fairly is part of being in community — and that resents being told what something costs by a company optimizing for extraction.

**"No ads, no data selling, user-owned PBC" lands hard.** In a city where the people building the surveillance machine also live under it, Evryn's structural privacy model isn't just a feature — it's a political statement. The planned Swiss Foundation for trust data is a competitive differentiator that Seattle's progressive, technically literate population understands and values.

**"Help us not need institutional capital" is Seattle's language.** Despite 75+ VC firms, the culture is skeptical of VC-driven scale-at-all-costs. Crowdfunding on StartEngine, the PBC structure, "users as owners" — this resonates harder in the PNW than anywhere else in the country. It's why Kickstarter resonated so hard with PNW creators. It's why Portland has more co-ops per capita than almost anywhere. The message "help us build something that can't be corrupted" isn't marketing here — it's speaking the local dialect.

## Part 3: The Spore Dynamic

This may be the most strategically important finding in this report.

### Seattle Is a Throughput City

Seattle has high inbound AND high outbound migration. People are constantly arriving and constantly leaving. This isn't a problem — it's a growth engine.

**Inbound flow:** 64,000 international migrants/year, plus domestic arrivals from California (#1 source), Spokane, San Francisco, Houston, DC, and Los Angeles.

**Outbound flow:** Net negative domestic migration. Top destinations: Los Angeles (#1), San Francisco, Portland, Phoenix, Sacramento, Spokane, Idaho, Arizona, Texas, Carolinas, Tennessee. Primarily middle-class families and non-tech workers leaving due to cost of living.

### The Spore Mechanic

Every person who builds a relationship with Evryn in Seattle and then moves somewhere else becomes a seed in their new city.

**Outbound spores:** Someone leaves Seattle for Phoenix. They take their Evryn relationship with them. They arrive needing connections — a doctor, a plumber, a job, a friend. Evryn helps them *there.* They tell someone in Phoenix about Evryn. Evryn doesn't need to "launch" in Phoenix. She arrives with her users. And the outbound flow is going to specific, predictable places — LA, Portland, Phoenix, Sacramento. These aren't random scatterings; they're migration corridors that Evryn can ride.

**Inbound spores:** An Indian tech worker arrives in Seattle with zero local network but deep connections in Bangalore. A Californian moves up with SF contacts. Evryn helps them in Seattle, but their trust graph extends back to where they came from. When someone in their origin network needs a connection in Seattle — or when someone in Seattle needs a connection in India or San Francisco — the network is already there. The trust graph becomes transnational not through strategic expansion but through the organic movement of people.

**The math:** If Evryn acquires 1,000 active users in Seattle and the city's net domestic out-migration rate holds, roughly 50-100 of those users leave for other cities each year — each one arriving in a new place needing exactly what Evryn provides. Over 3 years, that's 150-300 seeds planted in a dozen cities, each with an existing Evryn relationship and a reason to evangelize. This is organic geographic expansion that costs nothing.

**Strategic implication:** Seattle isn't just a launch city. It's the highest-throughput launchpad available — a city where people constantly arrive needing help and constantly leave carrying Evryn with them. Every dollar invested in Seattle user acquisition has a multiplier effect on national (and eventually international) expansion.

## Part 4: Community Ecosystem Map

Detailed map of Seattle's communities, with an eye toward where Evryn fits, where the gatekeeper opportunities are, and where the whisper cascade has the most fuel.

**Density:** Medium. Concentrated around a few key institutions. Growing — Washington State recently increased its Motion Picture Competitiveness Program from $3.5M to $15M annually.

**Anchor institutions:**
- **SIFF (Seattle International Film Festival):** One of the largest in the world. Oscar-qualifying. The anchor institution for Seattle film.
- **Northwest Film Forum (NWFF):** Capitol Hill nonprofit cinema. Nearly 1,000 members. Aids 250 filmmakers in producing ~80 films/year. 60+ workshops annually. Celebrated its 30th anniversary in 2025.
- **Women in Film Seattle:** One of 50 WIFTI chapters globally. Runs the Post Alley Film Festival at SIFF.
- **Blanket Fort Films:** Nonprofit empowering LGBTQ, POC, and women filmmakers through free access to education and gear.
- **Longhouse Media:** Indigenous media arts. Curates the Indigenous Showcase at NWFF since 2015.

**Evryn's position:** Film is Evryn's ignition point via Mark. The scene is small enough that Evryn can become the connective tissue quickly. The structural gap is clear: no central matchmaker for filmmaker-to-DP, actor-to-project, producer-to-screenwriter connections.

**Gatekeeper potential:** Mark (pilot, live this week). Megan's connections to indie producers, department heads, casting. SIFF and NWFF leadership.

### Tech / Startup

**Density:** Very high. One of the densest in the US.

**Key organizations:**
- **Alliance of Angels:** Largest PNW angel group. $10M+/year into 20+ companies. Since 1997: $100M+ into 200+ companies.
- **Founders' Co-op:** Most active pre-seed fund. 40+ first checks in 2025.
- **Madrona Venture Group:** Leading institutional VC.
- **Foundations:** Invite-only, in-person community of tech founders. The existence of an invite-only community is itself evidence that open networking doesn't work.

**Key events:**
- **Seattle Tech Week:** 4th year in 2026, nearly 200 events. The annual tentpole.

**Gatekeeper potential:** Accelerator directors, prominent angels, AI House leadership. These people get hundreds of pitches and connection requests.

### Food / Restaurant

**Density:** High, but informal and relationship-driven. Punches above its weight nationally (12 James Beard nominees in 2025).

For newcomers, there's no efficient way in. Evryn solves the "I just opened a restaurant and need to find a pastry chef and a local mushroom forager" problem.

**Gatekeeper potential:** Mark may have connections here (possibly Tom from Canlis — one of Seattle's most iconic fine-dining institutions). Top chefs and restaurant group leaders get bombarded with vendor pitches, job seekers, media requests. This is a sector where a lunch with Mark could open doors.

### Music

**Key institutions:**

### Fitness / Wellness

**Density:** Medium-high for boutique fitness, fragmented for practitioners.

**Key insights:**

One instructor who loves Evryn could cascade across multiple studios and hundreds of students.

**Evryn's position:** The fitness community is siloed — CrossFit doesn't talk to yoga, yoga doesn't talk to running, nobody talks to wellness practitioners. Justin's decade at Ballard Health Club and warm connections to fitness instructors is an entry point. The butterfly/cult-following dynamic is a near-perfect whisper cascade fuel source.

### Cross-Cutting Observation

The community ecosystem research produced one finding that appeared independently in every single sector:

**There is no dominant digital tool for community-level connection in any of Seattle's ecosystems.** People find each other through in-person events and word-of-mouth. Meetup.com comes closest but it's event-centric, not relationship-centric. The communities are warm internally but have high entry barriers for newcomers. Every ecosystem has the same structural gap: dense networks that don't talk to each other, and no lightweight way for someone new to find their way in.

This is what Evryn was built for.

## Part 6: Trust-Based Pricing and Cultural Resonance

This section maps the specific cultural dynamics that make Evryn's model resonate in Seattle more than it might elsewhere.

### "Pay What's Fair" Culture

Seattle has a deeply embedded "pay what you feel is right" ethos:
- **REI** was born here (a co-op, member-owned).
- Strong **farmers market culture** — direct relationships between producer and consumer, fair pricing as part of the relationship.
- **"Support local" identity** — people actively choose to pay more for local over cheap-and-corporate.
- **Progressive economic experimentation** — one of the first major cities to push $15 minimum wage.
- **Flying Bike Cooperative Brewery** — a co-op brewpub. The cooperative model has cultural cache here.

Trust-based pricing doesn't just work in Seattle — it speaks the local language. "Pay what you believe is fair for this connection" maps to a city that already thinks in terms of "what's the right price for the relationship I want to have with this business?"

### Anti-Surveillance Sentiment

Seattle houses the companies that built surveillance capitalism. Its population understands what that means:
- Amazon employees know what their company does with data.
- Microsoft employees know what platform economics look like from the inside.
- The broader population *lives under* the systems these companies built.

Evryn's "no ads, no data selling, never" model hits different here. It's not a marketing line — it's an answer to a wound this city carries. The planned Swiss Foundation for trust data is a competitive differentiator that technically literate Seattleites can evaluate and appreciate.

### Anti-VC Ownership Model

Despite 75+ local VC firms, the culture is skeptical:
- "Help us not need institutional capital" resonates with people who've watched VC-backed companies promise to change the world and then optimize for extraction.
- The PBC structure, crowdfunding on StartEngine, and "users as owners" framing play better in the PNW than anywhere else in the country.
- Portland amplifies this: more co-ops per capita, worker-owned restaurants, the Portland Alliance of Worker Cooperatives.
- The PNW shared its love of Kickstarter not because the region lacks money, but because the region values participating in building something together.

### What This Opens

These cultural resonances aren't just "nice to have" — they're growth mechanics:
- **Trust-based pricing creates word-of-mouth.** "I paid what I thought was fair and Evryn just... accepted it." That story gets told at dinner parties. It's novel enough to be remarkable and aligned enough with local values to feel validating.
- **Anti-surveillance positioning is a competitive moat.** In a city where consumer AI trust is actively declining (53% of dating app users worried about AI nationally), Evryn's structural privacy architecture is a real differentiator.
- **User ownership creates evangelists.** Users with skin in the game (pre-purchases, crowdfunding) become ambassadors. In a city with strong cooperative values, the "co-owner" identity carries social weight.

## Part 7: The I-5 Corridor — Portland and Beyond

### Portland: Not an Expansion, an Inevitability

If Evryn works in Seattle, Portland isn't a launch to plan — it's a destination the network reaches on its own.

**The creative scenes are already one ecosystem:**
- **IATSE Local 488** covers both Oregon and Washington — same union, same crew pool, same labor market. The film community is literally one market.
- **Sub Pop Records** (Seattle), **Kill Rock Stars** (Olympia/Portland), **K Records** (Olympia) — the music scene has always been one ecosystem with three poles.
- **Amtrak Cascades** hit record ridership in 2025: 951,397 passengers, 6 daily round trips. The cities are physically connected and getting more so.
- **Relay Restaurant Group** (Rachel Yang and Seif Chirchi) operates in both cities — one of several cross-city food businesses.
- Touring musicians play both cities on every West Coast run. Artists show in both cities. Filmmakers work wherever the production is.

**Portland's values amplify Evryn's model:**
- Stronger cooperative tradition than Seattle: worker-owned co-ops, **People's Food Co-op** (3,000+ member-owners since 1970), **Pidgin Cooperative** (worker-owned restaurant, opened 2025), **Portland Alliance of Worker Cooperatives**.
- Stronger indie publishing: **Powell's City of Books** (world's largest independent bookstore), **IPRC** (Independent Publishing Resource Center, 9,000+ item zine library), **Microcosm Publishing** (30th anniversary in 2025).
- The "Keep Portland Weird" ethos and Seattle's grunge/Sub Pop legacy are expressions of the same cultural impulse: anti-corporate, DIY, authenticity over scale.

**Portland's current state creates opportunity:**
- The corporate economy is struggling: 8,800 jobs lost in 2025 (4th worst nationally), Intel cut 2,400 Oregon jobs, Nike cutting tech workers, office leasing at lowest level on record.
- But the creative and community infrastructure remains strong — arguably concentrated as some corporate energy departed. Vibrant neighborhood scenes (Alberta Arts, Hawthorne, Pearl, Jade District), food cart culture, robust literary community.
- People in Portland need trusted connections for the same reasons people in Seattle do — and the economic struggles make it more urgent.

**How Evryn arrives in Portland:**
- Outbound spores: Seattleites moving to Portland (a top domestic migration destination from Seattle) take Evryn with them.
- Film crew: IATSE 488 workers who use Evryn on a Seattle production talk about it on a Portland production.
- Music: A musician who loves Evryn plays a Portland show and tells other musicians.
- Mark and Megan's connections: Both know people in Portland's creative scene.

The whisper cascade follows existing network lines south on I-5. Evryn doesn't launch in Portland. She *arrives.*

### The Wider Corridor

**Tacoma (30 minutes south):**
- Becoming a serious arts city. **Tacoma Creates** program distributed $6.4M to 80+ arts/culture/science organizations for 2025-2026 (reauthorized through 2032). Nonprofit arts sector impact: nearly $163M.
- Increasingly an affordability pressure valve for Seattle-based creatives and artists.
- Connected via I-5 and Sound Transit. Close enough to be part of the same community.

**Bellingham (90 minutes north):**
- Growing as a tech hub: projected 20% annual tech growth, potentially WA's third-largest tech hub.
- 24% below national average cost of living (15% cheaper than Seattle).
- Attracting remote workers and creatives priced out of Seattle. ~600 STEM graduates annually from Western Washington University.
- **Make.Shift Art Space:** 12 affordable art studios, 6 band practice spaces. The affordability play for artists.

**Olympia:**
- Historical epicenter of PNW DIY/punk/riot grrrl culture. Evergreen State College continues to feed alternative culture. More living legacy than growth story, but culturally significant.

**Bend, OR:**
- Outdoor and music culture hub. Strong community identity. More geographically isolated (3.5 hours from Portland), but culturally connected.

### The Corridor Strategy

None of these cities require separate launches. The I-5 corridor — Seattle to Portland, with Tacoma, Olympia, and Bellingham as nodes — functions as one cultural ecosystem with local flavors. Evryn spreads along it the same way music, film, and food culture already spread: organically, through the people who move between these places.

Evryn's launch strategy for the corridor is simply: win Seattle, let the network do the rest, and be ready when it arrives. The only strategic consideration is ensuring that when someone in Portland or Tacoma or Bellingham encounters Evryn, the experience is just as good as it is in Seattle — which, given that Evryn is digital and personal, it will be.

## Part 8: Implications and Recommendations

### The Big Picture

Seattle is not just a viable launch market — it may be the *ideal* launch market for what Evryn is. The combination of:
- Acute loneliness (worst depression among large metros, Freeze as structural barrier)
- Massive transplant population (needing everything, knowing nobody)
- Tech displacement (skilled people needing new connections, with money to pay)
- Cross-domain lives in a mid-size city (one person, many communities, no connecting tissue)
- Cultural alignment with trust-based pricing and anti-corporate ownership
- High-throughput migration (spore dynamic for organic expansion)

...creates a market where Evryn doesn't have to convince anyone they need trusted connections. They already know they do. They've been trying to solve it with events and apps and meetups and in-person dating gatherings. Nothing has worked. Evryn works because she's a fundamentally different tool — personal, persistent, cross-domain, trust-first.

### Strategic Priorities

1. **Film is the ignition point.** Mark live this week. Megan's connections for gatekeeper #2. SIFF for v0.3 presence. This is already the plan and it's right.

2. **The transplant/newcomer angle is the broadest water.** Once Evryn has a web app (v0.3) and can serve users directly, positioning Evryn as "the thing you need when you move to Seattle" taps into the 64,000 international migrants and constant domestic inbound. The Chamber of Connection validated the market — and demonstrated that events aren't the answer.

3. **Tech displacement is the highest-value water.** These are people with money, networks, and acute need. They also tend to be early adopters of new tools. If Evryn helps a few displaced tech workers find their next opportunity, that story spreads fast through the tech community.

4. **Cross-domain matching is the unique value.** Nobody else is doing this. Not in Seattle, not anywhere. This is what makes Evryn's Seattle story different from "another networking app" — she sees across all of your life.

5. **The fitness butterfly network is a high-leverage whisper cascade seed.** Justin's warm connections to fitness instructors who've scattered across the city, each with loyal followings, is a remarkably efficient distribution channel. Not a gatekeeper play — a whisper cascade play.

6. **A lunch with Mark should happen soon.** His broader network (restaurant world, Chihuly, commercial/corporate connections) could map entry points into food, art, and business communities that are otherwise hard to reach from the outside.

7. **Portland is the first organic expansion.** Don't plan it. Don't launch it. Just be ready when it arrives. It will come through film crew, musicians, and people who move.

### What's Different About This Market

Most startups launching in a new city have to educate the market about why the problem exists. Evryn doesn't. Seattle already knows the problem exists. The city has been publicly agonizing about the Freeze, the loneliness epidemic, the social isolation of transplants, the dating desert, and the inability to make friends for years. The Surgeon General issued an advisory. The city launched programs. Amazon and Microsoft partnered with a nonprofit to try to help. The data is in every local news outlet.

The question isn't "does Seattle need Evryn?" The question is "can Evryn deliver?" The answer to that question is being built right now.
